# Business Model Canvas – EdGame v3.0

> **Snapshot of the company at full operational scale (Year 5)**
> EdGame is an EdTech platform that augments traditional assessment and homework with immersive, game based learning environments focused on **Math and Science** with specific topics related to Saudi Curriculum. We capture behavioral data behind the scenes that traditional assessments miss such as students mindset solving problems, collaboration, and behave under pressure. Providing educators a complete picture of student capabilities based on real time data monitoring.
>
> **Core insight**: Traditional assessments capture only correctness. EdGame captures *why* students get answers wrong; guessing patterns, frustration points, and which concepts need reteaching. This is the insight that Classcraft's June 2024 shutdown left a massive gap for.
>
> **Strategic approach**: Beachhead → Expand → Dominate. Start narrow (Math + Science correlated to the specific curriculum of KAUST & NEOM SCHOOL, GCC + US Charter schools), prove value, then expand.

## 1. Customer Segments

### Phase 1 Beachhead (Year 1–2): Saudi Flagship + GCC Private/International Schools

| Segment | Description | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Saudi Flagship Schools (primary): KAUST School + NEOM Schools** | High-innovation K–12 environments with fast feedback cycles and direct access through the KAUST network; ideal for controlled pilots and rapid iteration | 10–15 pilots → 5–8 paid by Year 1 end |
| **GCC Private/International Schools (secondary)** | Private/international K–12 schools across the GCC (incl. KSA, UAE, Qatar) with English-medium instruction and openness to innovation pilots (IB/IGCSE/American programs) | 25–40 pilots → 10–18 paid by Year 1 end |
| **Individual Math/Science Teachers (PLG)** | Teachers adopting via self-serve onboarding; act as internal champions that drive school-level pilots and procurement | 1,000 free → 100 paid by Year 1 end |

**Why Saudi Flagship First:** The KAUST and NEOM school ecosystems enable the fastest iteration loop (weekly feedback), easier controlled pilots, and strong local credibility. Proximity allows frequent in-person observation and faster co-design with teachers. We use these early outcomes to scale into GCC private/international schools with proven efficacy and implementation playbooks.

**Assumptions behind targets (Year 1):** pilot duration 6–10 weeks; pilot→paid conversion 30% (Average number for this kind of project) for flagship schools and 25% for broader GCC; teacher PLG is primarily a top-of-funnel channel to seed school pilots.

## 4. Customer Relationships

| Relationship Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Self-serve (individual teachers)** | Teachers can sign up, choose Math or Science, select a game environment, and assign a first activity in under 5 minutes. In-app onboarding, templates, and guided setup reduce friction; optional AI-assisted customization helps teachers adapt challenges to their lesson plan. |
| **School onboarding (high-touch → scalable)** | Phase 1 uses founder-led onboarding and co-design with a designated school implementation lead. Runs as a structured **6–10 week pilot** with weekly check-ins and a clear success rubric. In Phase 2, onboarding transitions to an Implementation Manager playbook with repeatable training kits and rollout checklists. |
| **Evidence & analytics-driven retention** | Weekly “top insights” summaries and dashboards help teachers plan reteaching and interventions (e.g., misconception clusters, repeated error types, time-to-solve trends, hint dependence). Leadership receives periodic impact reviews to support renewal and expansion decisions. |
| **Community & competitions (Phase 2+)** | Optional school-to-school STEM challenges and teacher community programming (webinars, office hours, shared templates). Designed to strengthen engagement and create a durable usage habit beyond a single classroom. |

### Critical Assumptions (Phase 1–2)

1) **Schools will enable actionable feedback loops**  
We assume pilot schools and teachers will provide **regular, high quality feedback** (e.g., weekly check-ins, classroom observations, feature requests, and outcome expectations) that enables rapid iteration of EdGame’s gameplay, analytics, and lesson alignment.  
How we test in Phase 1: Define a weekly feedback cadence with named school owners, capture feedback in a shared log, and ship at least one iteration per week to validate feedback quality and speed.

2) **Permission to analyze student data (most critical)**  
We assume schools and guardians will allow **school-approved** collection and analysis of student learning data generated during gameplay (e.g., attempts, time-to-solve, hint usage, error types, progress signals). This is **the most critical assumption** because EdGame’s differentiation depends on generating insights beyond correctness.  
How we test in Phase 1: Secure written pilot consent terms, run a small cohort with explicit approvals, and confirm which data fields are permitted for analysis and reporting.

3) **Regulatory feasibility for data collection**  
We assume the legal and regulatory environment in our target markets (starting with Saudi/GCC) will allow collection and processing of these data **with appropriate safeguards** (e.g., consent where required, data minimization, anonymization/pseudonymization, access controls, retention limits, and school-approved policies), where applicable.  
How we test in Phase 1: Produce a one-page data governance summary for the pilot, validate it with school compliance/admin, and implement minimum safeguards (access controls, retention rules, and anonymized reporting) before scaling.

## 7. Key Activities

| Activity | Description |
|---|---|
| **Game environment development (puzzle + competition)** | Build 3 polished, curriculum-aligned Math/Science environments in Phase 1 (puzzles + competitive challenges), expanding to ~12 environments in Phase 2. Co-design with pilot teachers to map content to specific modules currently taught and to validate engagement and classroom fit. |
| **Game loop & engagement design** | Define and iterate the core loop (challenge → feedback → progression → competition) so sessions are short (10–15 minutes), repeatable, and classroom-safe. Validate retention drivers (completion rate, replay, time-on-task, peer challenge uptake) during pilots. |
| **Behavioral analytics & reporting** | Ship an MVP analytics module with ~15 core signals that answer “What do I do Monday morning?” (misconceptions vs guessing, where students get stuck, hint dependence, time-to-solve patterns). Expand the signal library to 30+ in Phase 2, while keeping dashboards curated and actionable. |
| **Design system & MVP UX** | Create and apply the design guide (UI kit, components, typography, colors) to keep the MVP consistent across environments and to accelerate iteration as new modules are added. |
| **Platform engineering & hosting** | Deliver a web-first platform (e.g., Supabase + Vercel + Phaser), including authentication, telemetry pipelines, secure data storage, and monitoring. Add mobile and multiplayer infrastructure in Phase 2 once usage patterns justify it. |
| **Founder-led pilots & sales (Phase 1)** | Secure Saudi flagship + GCC pilots through direct demos and relationships. Run structured 6–10 week pilots with weekly check-ins, clear success criteria, and end-of-pilot impact reviews. Transition to repeatable playbooks and an AE/CS motion in Phase 2. |
| **Evidence generation & efficacy research** | Build an evidence package from pilots (teacher feedback, usage metrics, learning proxies, time saved). Pursue IRB/partner study when required for expansion into more regulated/public systems and larger-scale procurement (international expansion later). |
| **Data governance & compliance (critical)** | Define pilot consent language, data minimization, role-based access controls, anonymized/pseudonymized reporting, and retention policies. Ensure schools approve what data are collected, how insights are used, and whether any parent-facing views are permitted. |

### Key Assumptions (enabling these activities)

1) **Pilot schools enable fast feedback**  
We assume pilot schools will commit to a weekly feedback cadence (teachers + implementation lead) so we can iterate gameplay, UX, and curriculum alignment quickly.

2) **Legal and school-approved MVP deployment**  
We assume the MVP can be deployed in target schools without legal blockers, and that schools will approve the pilot scope, tooling, and data handling terms before launch.

3) **Permission to collect and analyze student data (most critical)**  
We assume schools and guardians will allow school-approved collection and analysis of student gameplay data needed for insights (e.g., attempts, time-to-solve, hint usage, error types, progress signals). This is critical to deliver our differentiated analytics.

4) **Regulatory compliance is achievable with safeguards**  
We assume local regulations will allow processing of student data when safeguards are in place (consent where required, data minimization, pseudonymization/anonymization, role-based access, retention limits, and auditability).

5) **School IT integration is feasible**  
We assume schools can support required technical setup (accounts/class setup, network access, device/browser compatibility) so the web-first MVP runs reliably during pilots.

6) **Teachers can adopt a lightweight workflow**  
We assume teachers can integrate a 10–15 minute game assignment into existing routines and will find the “top insights” reports actionable enough to keep using the tool.

7) **Engagement loop drives repeat usage**  
We assume puzzle + competition mechanics create repeat sessions without undermining learning focus, and that competition features can be designed in a classroom-appropriate way.

8) **Analytics signals are interpretable and trusted**  
We assume the initial signal set can be validated with teachers as meaningful and not misleading, supporting real decisions (reteaching, grouping, intervention).

9) **Hosting and reliability meet school expectations**  
We assume MVP reliability and privacy posture (uptime, latency, secure storage) are sufficient to avoid trust-breaking failures during pilots.

10) **Pilot evidence supports expansion**  
We assume pilot outcomes will generate a credible evidence package that supports broader GCC rollout and later international/public-system expansion.
